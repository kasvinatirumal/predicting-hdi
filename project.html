Research Questions and Summary

  1. How have a countrys per capita Gross Domestic Product (GDP), carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions, and Human Development Index (HDI) changed from 2010 to 2022? How have the top countries in each metric changed from 2010 to 2022?

    Median per capita GDP and HDI showed a general increasing trend from 2010 to 2022 across all continents. Median per capita CO2 emissions either remained relatively stable or experienced slight increases, with the exception of Europe where per capita CO2 emissions declined over the time period.

    For per capita GDP, Luxembourg, Norway and Switzerland were in the top 5 for both years. While Qatar and San Marino were previously ranked among the top 5 in 2010, they were replaced by Ireland and Singapore in 2022. For per capita CO2 emissions, Qatar, Trinidad and Tobago, and the United Arab Emirates remained in the top 5, with Kuwait and Aruba being replaced by Bahrain and Saudi Arabia in 2022. For HDI, Switzerland, Norway and Iceland remained in the top 5, with Germany and Austrailia being replaced by Denmark and Sweden in 2022.

    We also found that countries with higher GDP per capita tend to higher CO2 emissions per capita and HDI.

  2. Is there a difference in HDI across different per capita CO2 emission levels (Low, Medium, High) in 2022?

    We found significant variaton in HDI across different per capita CO2 emission levels, as evidenced by the extremely small p-value and large F-statistic from the one-way ANOVA test conducted.

  3. How well do economic indicators predict HDI, and which indicators are the most important?

    Economic indicators alone are able to reliably predict HDI well, with the best XGBoost model achieving a root mean squared error (RMSE) score of 0.024. Given that HDI values range from 0 and 1, an small RMSE of 0.024 indicates strong predictive performance. The most important indictors for predicting HDI were identified to be GDP per capita, Investment, and Unemployment Rate.

  4. Does including per capita CO2 emissions as a predictor improve accuracy of HDI predictions?

    Including per capita CO2 emissions as a predicor increased HDI prediction accuracy, as the RMSE of the best XGBoost model decreased from 0.024 to 0.021. This represents a 12.5% improvement in predictive accuracy for HDI.

Data Setting and Methods

About the Data

For this project, the Economic Indicators dataset was the primary dataset used, and was retrieved from the International Monetary Fund (IMF) website. This dataset contains 8624 rows and 60 columns, with each countrys data being represented by 44 rows, each corresponding to a unique economic indicator or the same indicator measured in different units. The dataset contains the following columns: WEO Country Code, ISO, WEO Subject Code, Country, Subject Descriptor, Subject Notes, Units, Scale, Country/Series-specific Notes, Years 1980 to 2029 (inclusive), and Estimates Start After.

The other 4 datasets were sourced from Our World in Data. The Per Capita CO2 Emissions, Human Development Index and Unemployment Rate datasets contained multiple rows per country, with a varying number of rows depending on the available time frame for the respective statistic of that country. Each row in the Continents dataset corresponds to a unique country. The Human Development Index, Unemployment Rate and Continents datasets share the same 4 columns: Entity, Code, Year, and their respective statistics, whereas the Per Capita CO2 Emissions dataset includes the same columns except for Code.

Below are links to the datasets used:

  1. Economic Indicators: https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WEO/weo-database/2024/October
  2. Per Capita CO2 Emissions: https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/co-emissions-per-capita
  3. Human Development Index: https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/human-development-index
  4. Unemployment Rate: https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/unemployment-rate
  5. Continents: https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/continents-according-to-our-world-in-data

Data Preprocessing Plan

  1. The Economic Indicators dataset, henceforth referred to as the main dataset, contains 44 rows of indicator information per country. To simplify analysis, the following nine economic indicators were selected based on macroeconomic knowledge: GDP per capita NGDPDPC, Investment NID_NGDP, Gross National Savings NGSD_NGDP, Inflation PCPIPCH, Volume of Imports TM_RPCH, Volume of Exports TX_RPCH, Unemployment Rate LUR, Population LP, and Implied Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) Conversion Rate PPPEX. Furthermore, indicators recorded in multiple units were standardized for consistency across countries.

  2. The main dataset includes projections through 2029. While we considered excluding all estimated years, this would have removed too many observations, especially for smaller countries with estimates starting as early as 2003. To maximize data completeness, the range of analysis was chosen to be from the year 2010 to 2022, as most countries have non-estimated data through 2022.

  3. The main dataset is structured with years as columns and indicators as the rows which complicates analysis. To solve this, the dataset will be reshaped so that years become rows and indicator values become columns.

  4. The main dataset will be merged with the other 4 datasets using a left join. The Per Capita CO2 Emissions, Human Development Index, and Unemployment Rate datasets are merged with the main dataset on Country and Year, while the Continents dataset is merged on only Country.

  5. After completing visualizations for research question 1, missing values with the main dataset will be imputed using the median of the continent that each country belongs to for the given year.

def clean_merge_and_pivot(df):    
    """
    Takes in a dataset df and filters it to only include rows
    with the following nine economic indicators found under the
    "WEO Subject Code" column: "NGDPDPC", "NID_NGDP", "NGSD_NGDP",
    "PCPIPCH", "TM_RPCH", "TX_RPCH", "LUR", "LP", and "PPPEX".
    The dataset is then transformed into a long format with "Year"
    as a single column, and then pivoted back to a wide format where
    each WEO Subject Code and their respective values become separate
    columns. The resulting dataset with its index reset is then returned. 
    Assumes that the given df includes the columns "WEO Subject Code",
    "ISO", "Country", and multiple year columns.
    """
    codes = ["NGDPDPC", "NID_NGDP", "NGSD_NGDP", "PCPIPCH", "TM_RPCH",
             "TX_RPCH", "LUR", "LP", "PPPEX"] 
    # filters the dataset to only include rows with the WEO Subject Codes
    # listed in codes
    df = df.loc[df["WEO Subject Code"].isin(codes), :]

    # sets up relevant years as string for analysis
    years = [str(year) for year in range(2010,2023)]
    years_str = [year for year in years if year in df.columns]
    
    # converts the dataset from wide to long format
    df_melted = df.melt(
        id_vars=["ISO","Country","WEO Subject Code"],
        value_vars=years_str,
        var_name="Year", value_name="Value"
    )

    # converts the dataset from long to wide format
    df_pivoted = df_melted.pivot(
        index=["ISO","Country","Year"], 
        columns="WEO Subject Code", values="Value"
    )
    return df_pivoted.reset_index() # resets index

def convert_numeric(df):
    """
    Takes in a dataset df, and processes all columns starting from
    the third columns onwards. Non-numeric characters from values
    within these columns are removed, and converted into a numeric
    data type. Values that cannot be converted to a numeric data 
    type are set to NaN. The resulting dataset is returned. 
    Assumes that the given df has at least 3 columns.
    """
    for col in df.columns[2:]: 
        # strips non-numeric characters from the column values
        df[col] = df[col].replace("[^0-9.]", "", regex=True)
        df[col] = pd.to_numeric(df[col], errors="coerce")
    return df

def combine_datasets(df1, df2, l_keys, r_keys=None):
    """
    Takes in two dataset, df1 and df2, and left join keys l_keys,
    and optional right join keys r_keys. The datasets df1 and
    df2 are merged using a left join on the given l_keys and
    r_keys. If r_keys is not provided, we assume that the left
    and right join keys are identical. The resulting merged dataset
    with the "Entity" column dropped is returned.
    Assumes that the df1 and df2 contains the columns listed in
    l_keys and r_keys respecitevely. If r_keys was not provided,
    assumes that df2 contains the columns listed in l_keys.
    """
    if r_keys is None:
        r_keys = l_keys
    return pd.merge(df1, df2, how="left", left_on=l_keys, right_on=r_keys).drop(columns="Entity")

Data Dictionary

The resulting econ_df is represented as a pandas DataFrame with the following columns:

  • ISO is a unique code that indentifies each country.
  • Country is the name of the country.
  • Year is the calendar year in which data was recorded for a country.
  • LP is the total population of a country.
  • NGDPDPC is the gross domestic product per capita, expressed in current US dollars.
  • NGSD_NGDP is a ratio of gross national savings in current local currency and GDP in current local currency.
  • NID_NGDP is a ratio of total investment in current local currency and GDP in current local currency
  • PCPIPCH is inflation rate, measured as the year-on-year percentage change in average consumer prices.
  • PPPEX is the implied PPP conversion rate, expressed in national currency per current international dollar.
  • TM_RPCH is the volume of imports of good and services.
  • TX_RPCH is the volume of exports of good and services.
  • CO2 is the per capita annual carbon dixoide emissions, measured in metric tonnes.
  • HDI is the human development index, which is a composite measure of human development in a country.
  • UR is the unemployment rate of a country.
  • Continent is the continent that a country belongs to.

def plot_interactive_viz(df, col, title, file_name, legend=None):
    """
    Takes in a dataset df, column col, title, file_name and an optional 
    legend, creates an interactive choropleth map of the values from
    the given col for each country, and saves the plot as an html file.
    The map includes an animation of the data from the year 2010 to 2022
    (inclusive). A diverging color scale of BrBG is used, and the map
    is titled using the given title.
    If a legend is provided, it is used to label the color scale. If not,
    the color scale will share the same label as the given col.
    The map is titled "Worldwide Per Capita GDP (2010-2022)" (if Per
    Capita GDP is the given col).
    Assumes that the given dataset has the columns "Country", "Year",
    and the given col.
    Assumes the given file_name contains no spaces.
    """
    fig = px.choropleth(
        df,
        locations="Country",
        locationmode="country names",
        color=col,
        hover_name="Country",
        animation_frame="Year",
        color_continuous_scale=px.colors.diverging.BrBG,
        title=f"Worldwide {title} (2010-2022)"
    )
    if legend is not None:
        fig.update_layout(coloraxis_colorbar_title=legend)
    fig.write_html(f"{file_name}.html")

Similar with per capita GDP, Africa has the lowest per capita CO2 emissions.

Overall, we observe a strong link between economic development, environmental impact, and human development, where wealthier regions with higher per capita GDP, also had higher per capita CO2 emissions and HDI values. This also implies that it is more challenging for poorer countries, such as those in Africa, to attain higher HDI due to a lack of capital to strengthen their economies. Therefore, targeted policy intervention is essential to address the disparities between wealthier and poorer nations, so that human development can be improved in less affluent countries.

def plot_line_chart(df):
    """
    Takes in a dataset df, and plots three line charts displaying
    the median values of Per Capita GDP, CO2 emissions, and HDI by
    continent from 2010 to 2022 (inclusive). Each plot displays
    six separate lines, each representing a different continent.
    The charts are arranged on a 2x2 grid, with two plots on the
    top row, and one on the left side of the bottom row.
    The titles of the plot from top to bottom, left to right, include
    "Per Capita GDP($) by Continent", "Per Capita CO2 Emissions (tonnes)
    by Continent", and "HDI by Continent". All three plots have the
    same x-axis label named "Year", while the y-axes are labeled 
    "Median Per Capita GDP ($)", "Median Per Capita CO2 Emissions
    (tonnes)" and "Median HDI" respectively.
    The resulting 2x2 figure is displayed. 
    Assumes the given df contains the columns "NGDPDPC", "CO2", "HDI",
    "Year" and "Continent".
    """
    cols = ["NGDPDPC", "CO2", "HDI"]
    titles = ["Per Capita GDP ($)", "Per Capita CO2 Emissions (tonnes)", "HDI"]
    data = df.groupby(["Continent", "Year"])[cols].median().reset_index()

    fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(13.5, 8))
    # flatten array to iterate over it
    axes = axes.flatten()
    for ax, col, title in zip(axes[:3], cols, titles):
        sns.lineplot(data=data, x="Year", y=col, ax=ax, hue="Continent")
        ax.set(
            title=f'{title} by Continent',
            xlabel="Year", ylabel=f"Median {title}"
        )
    # remove empty subplot
    fig.delaxes(axes[3])
    fig.suptitle("Line Plot of Each Metric by Continent", fontsize=14)
    plt.tight_layout()

RQ2: Statistical Analysis of Variation in HDI Across CO2 Emission Levels

In this section, we will perform a hypothesis test to determine whether there is a statistically significant difference in HDI values across different per capita CO2 emission levels in 2022. The hypotheses are as follows:

  • Null hypothesis: HDI values do not differ across CO2 emission levels in 2022
  • Alternative hypothesis: HDI values differ across CO2 emission levels in 2022

We start by imputing missing values within our dataset econ_df, using the median values of their respective continent for the given year. Instead of dropping these rows, we opted to impute missing values as we wanted to perserve valuable information from other columns, which would be especially imoprtant when training our machine learning models.

In[19]:
def impute(df):
    """
    Takes in a dataset df, and imputes missing values within numerical
    columns of the dataset by replacing them with the median value of
    the continent that this country belongs to for that given year. 
    The resulting dataset is returned. 
    Assumes the given df contains the columns "Continent" and "Year".
    """
    numerical_cols = df.select_dtypes(include=[np.number])
    for col in numerical_cols:
        group = df.groupby(["Continent", "Year"])[col]
        df[col] = group.transform(lambda x: x.fillna(x.median()))
    return df

In[20]:
def categorize(df, col):
    """
    Takes in a dataset df and a column col, and creates a new
    column named "CO2_Level" (if the given col is CO2), then 
    categorizes values in the given col into three levels:
    Low, Medium or High. Values less than or equal to the 33th
    percentile are categorized as Low, values between the 33th
    and 66th percentile (inclusive) are categorized as Medium,
    and the rest are categorized as High.
    The resulting dataset with the new column is returned. 
    Assumes the given df contains the given col.
    """
    low = df[col].quantile(0.33)
    high = df[col].quantile(0.66)

    new_col = f"{col}_Level"
    df[new_col] = 'Low' 
    df.loc[(df[col] > low) & (df[col] <= high), new_col] = 'Medium'
    df.loc[df[col] > high, new_col] = 'High'
    return df

def one_way_anova(df, col):
    """
    Takes in a dataset df, and column col, and performs a one-way
    ANOVA test to compare the means of HDI values across different
    categories of the given column. The results of the ANOVA test
    is returned as a tuple that includes the F-statistic and p-value.
    Assumes the given df contains the column "HDI" and the given col.
    """
    samples = []
    for level, agg_values in df.groupby(col):
        hdi_values = agg_values["HDI"].values
        samples.append(hdi_values)
    return stats.f_oneway(*samples)


F_stat, p_val = one_way_anova(econ_2022, "CO2_Level")
print(f"F-statistic: {F_stat}")
print(f"P-value: {p_val}")

def rfe(X, y, model, name):
    """
    Performs recursive feature elimination (RFE) using a regression
    model of the given model type to select the top 5 features from
    the given feature matrix X and target vector y.
    The list of top 5 features and the RMSE of the models 
    predictions on the test set are then printed out using the
    given name on two separate lines, followed by a blank line.
    The list of top 5 features is returned.
    Assumes y_test is defined prior to running this function.
    """
    rfe = RFE(model, step=1, n_features_to_select=5, verbose=1)
    rfe.fit(X, y)
    rfe_features = X.columns[[r == 1 for r in rfe.ranking_]]
    
    print(f"{name} Features:", list(rfe_features))
    print(f"{name} RFE Error:", root_mean_squared_error(y_test, rfe.predict(X_test)))
    print()
    return list(rfe_features)

def cross_validation_xgb(features, X, X_train, X_test, y):
    """
    Takes in a list of features, and subsets the given matrices
    X, X_train and X_test to only include the given features.
    Hyperparamter tuning is then performed using cross-validation
    on an XGBoost regression model, with the following parameters
    being tuned: learning rate, maximum depth, minimum child weight,
    and column sample by tree. The model is trained on the subsetted
    X-train and given y. The best model is chosen based on the
    highest negative root mean squared error (RMSE) score. The mean
    score of the best model, the model's hyperparamters, and the most
    important features ranked by feature importance scores in
    descending order are printed. The trained XGBoost model with the
    tuned hyperparamters is returned. 
    """
    X = X[features]
    X_train = X_train[features]
    X_test = X_test[features]
    param_grid={
        "learning_rate"    : [0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.2, 0.3],
        "max_depth"        : [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10],
        "min_child_weight" : [1, 3, 6, 8, 10],
        "colsample_bytree" : [0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.8]
    }
    search = GridSearchCV(
        estimator=xgb.XGBRegressor(random_state=25),
        param_grid=param_grid,
        scoring="neg_root_mean_squared_error",
        n_jobs=-1, 
        verbose=0,
    )
    search.fit(X_train, y)
    print("Mean score for best model:", search.best_score_)
    reg = search.best_estimator_
    print("Best model:", reg)
    
    # Prints feature importance scores in descending order
    fi_scores = pd.Series(reg.feature_importances_, index=features).sort_values(ascending=False)
    print("Most Important Features:\n" + fi_scores.to_string())
    return reg

Implications and Limitations

From this project, we learned that per capita GDP, CO2 emissions, and HDI are positively correlated, and that both economic indicators and CO2 emissions serve as significant predictors of HDI.

The results of this project are valuable for those interested in improving a country's HDI, which includes academics, economists, policymakers, and local, state and governmental agencies. This is because stakeholders can use the predictive model to evaluate how changes in the values of different key predictors of HDI affect HDI, and select the optimal combination of predictor values that maximizes HDI while minimizing environmental impact and promoting economic growth. Policies can then be implemented to align real-word indicator values to match this optimal combination, which ultimately leads to a reliable improvement in HDI.

However, it is important to consider these results alongside the limitations of this analysis, which includes:

  1. Possible misinterpretation of results

    Our analysis revealed a moderate positive correlation between HDI and per capita CO2 emissions, suggesting that higher CO2 emissions are associated with higher HDI. While this may seem counterintuitive since we expect increased pollution to reduce quality of life, and thus lower HDI, this correlation actually reflects the indirect relationship between economic activity and CO2 emissions, not the intentional increase of emissions. Consequently, these findings could be misused by those seeking to harm the environment, potentially leading to policies that increase CO2 emissions under the false belief that they improve HDI.

  2. Reliability of data

    The main dataset used within this analysis, Economic Indicators, was sourced from the IMF website. This presents a limitation because the IMF works closely with governments worldwide to foster global monetary corporation. This monetary relationship incentivizes countries to misreport their data in ways that align with their economic interests, especially since these numbers affect the financial assistance they receive from the IMF. As a result, the dataset may be inaccurate or biased, which may have skewed the strength of correlation observed between per capita GDP, CO2 emissions and HDI. Consequently, policies derived from this analysis may not be as effective as projected. For future analysis, we will cross-check the IMF data with data from other sources such as the World Bank, to ensure that they are consistent and accurate.

  3. Completeness of data

    The Economic Indicators dataset contained a significant number of missing values, which were addressed through imputation, by using the median value of each continent for its respective year. However, it is important to consider that missing values were most prevalent among smaller and less developed countries. As a result, these countries should use the results of this analysis with caution, as the imputed data used to train the machine learning models may not best represent their economic and social trends. Conversely, this analysis is likely more accurate for developing and developed countries, since their data were typically more complete. For future analysis, instead of using the continents median for imputation, we will cross-check missing values with other databases to identify an exact value.

  4. Simplification of analysis

    Our analysis was simplified by selecting only nine indicators out of the 44 available within the original Economic Indicators dataset. This selection was somewhat biased, as it was based on personal macroeconomic knowledge. Thus, it is likely that there may have been other important predictors of HDI that were unintentionally excluded from the analysis, which diminishes the accuracy of our findings. Thus, for future analysis, we will attempt to consider all 44 indicators, and identify the actual 5 most important predictors of HDI from them. This eliminates the possibility of missing important variables that correlate with HDI.

  5. Absence of Year as a predictor in the final model

    When looking at the top 5 predictors of HDI identified by the XGBoost model that included CO2 as a predictor, it is important to note that Year was not selected as a top 5 predictor. Consequently, Year was excluded as a parameter for users to input when estimating HDI. However, given that we expect the values of economic indicators, CO2 emissions, and HDI to change on a year-to-year basis, it seems illogical to not include Year as a parameter. Hence, for future analysis, the importance of Year as a predictor will be evaluated, to better understand our model and strengthen the analysis within this project.
